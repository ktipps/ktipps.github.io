Making Finding a Book at Your Local Library Easier


Lead User Researcher & Designer

Duration: 1 Month


The Bottom-Line Up Front

Finding a Book at a Large Library is Not Easy

Large public libraries can feel monolithic if you who have never been there before. What floor do I go to? How do I get to the section I want? What the heck do all these Dewey Decimal numbers mean?

At the end of the day, people just want to find the book they want and checkout with it, especially if they need that book for a tight deadline like an academic project.


Library Goers Need Better Navigation Systems

We decided to investigate how we could improve the in-person navigation experience at the Carnegie Library’s main Pittsburgh branch and make it easier to physically navigate to your book.

From a variety of research methods, we ultimately discovered two key needs that people have are

  1. Clear directions to get to the section their book is in (even if it’s on another floor)
  2. A better way to locate the book on the shelf than the Dewey Decimal system

Bridging the Gap: AR Tech and Libraries

To fit users’ needs and give them a better way to find their book, we created a real-time map of the user’s local library.

We also took a page from bank check scanning technology and allowed users to hold up their phone and see where the book is on the shelf. Now, you can easily find your book.


Methods

Why These Methods

For this project, we were given tight timelines, and a budget of $0.00.

For our generative research, we had 24 hours to conduct the research and deliver a research report presentation. Given this tight timeline, we conducted seven intercept interviews followed by an interpretation session.

For our evaluative work, we typically had 72 hours to prototype and test the efficacy of those prototypes. As a result, we leveraged low-cost and fast methods like competitor analysis and usability studies with a think aloud protocol.


Key insights Along the Way


Match Users' Mental Models

It is not enough to include a floor plan layout with a ‘ you are here ’ indicator.

Users need feedforward and feedback about the status of where they are, and where their book is.


Narrow Project Scope

When facing tight deadlines, you need to narrow the scope of your project.

In mid-fidelity testing participants felt overwhelmed by too many features. Ultimately, we decided to focus on one core navigation feature.


The End Product




Closing Thoughts & Next Steps

This project was an exercise in rapid research, but if I had more time, I would love to dive into the complexity of this topic.

  • How would this proposed solution help library goers at smaller library branches?
  • How would this tool be integrated into the current county-wide website system?
  • How do librarians feel about the current navigation system or this solution?
  • What stakeholders or functional requirements have we not considered?


There are some of the questions I would love to explore given more time.
